New to game development?

Published on: 9/14/2024

Updated on: 10/18/2024

Learning Game Development

First, I'd like to give you some context, by the time I started diving into game development I was doing the internships of my vocational training, however, during my studies I also had the chance to approach the topic a little bit. We implemented an animation loop with double buffer to paint little games in the window of an application, and we also implemented very little games as proof of concept in the terminal using Java and a bit of C. However, as I explained in the summary, most of our work was more related to manage resources, It could be clients, teams in a tournament, inventory from a store and we designed systems capable of meeting the requirements that were proposed in the exercises. This is quite useful in general, and knowing this can give you an edge when it comes to game programming as well, you will know how to approach the persistence of the data, design wisely the architecture of the game, even approach online game development, but gameplay and graphic programming is a topic that for me and my class mates was alien back then. In my first day I was given a document with a lot of resources, most of them aimed to people with background in graphics, I decided to steer away from those and focus more on content that would allow me take advantage of my background. so here's the list of resources that helped me and might help you, I'd say, even if you don't have background in programming, the resources I'm about to tell you, will help you understand not only game programming but also they are good introductions to programming in general.

Create with Code: Unity

This course is free, and you will be provided with the assets required to make the games that the course proposes. It will guide you through basic concepts like player controls, basic game play, FX, user interfaces and more. The aspect I enjoyed the most is that It will give you extra challenges you have to figure out on your own and even room to include your own ideas if you desire. Not only this, it will provide you useful information if you want to create your own prototypes, not many people talk about the importance of the previous phase where you can take paper and pen and layout your ideas, this might not be discussed directly, but there's hints about it, for example they explain the purpose of the Game Design Document and encourage you to make your own. Another question worth mentioning is that the course is only a part of what Unity offers for people interested in learning, there are different pathways offered in their platform and a lot of other courses that covers topics from mobile development and monetization to talks about the video game industry for people that considers starting a career on it, here the link to the course mentioned.
